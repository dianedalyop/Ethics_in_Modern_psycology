Overview

Ethics in Modern Psychology: The aim of this project is to present a balanced viewpoint on professional practice standards. I am not a medical professional, and the perspectives shared here and in the accompanying video are based on academic research and a review of various sources.

Ethical Issues in Freud's Work

Sigmund Freud's contributions to psychology are foundational, but many of his methods and practices raise significant ethical concerns when viewed through a modern lens.

  • Lack of informed consent: Freud often analyzed patients, including children, without modern consent standards.
  • Patient confidentiality: Many of Freud’s case studies revealed identifiable personal information.
  • Bias and interpretation: Critics argue that Freud interpreted patient behavior through his own theoretical lens, raising ethical concerns about objectivity.
  • Therapist–patient boundaries: Freud’s relationships with patients blurred professional boundaries that today would be considered inappropriate.

Ethical Issues in Adler's Work

Alfred Adler, founder of Individual Psychology, took a more socially-oriented and optimistic approach than Freud. However, some aspects of his work still bring up ethical considerations.

  • Early case documentation: Like many early psychologists, Adler did not follow modern standards for confidentiality when publishing case studies.
  • Cultural assumptions: Some of Adler’s interpretations about social roles and "inferiority complexes" may appear overly generalized or culturally biased today.
  • Lack of standardized methods: Adler’s therapeutic approach was more intuitive than empirical, raising concerns about reliability and patient protection.

Historical Case Studies

  • Milgram Obedience Study – Ethical issues around stress, deception, and lack of consent.
  • Stanford Prison Experiment – Harm, power abuse, lack of oversight.
  • Little Albert – No informed consent, long-term harm, unethical conditioning of a child.

Modern Ethical Standards

Modern psychology ethics are guided by frameworks such as the APA's Ethical Principles and Code of Conduct. These include:

  • Informed consent
  • Right to withdraw
  • Protection from harm
  • Confidentiality
  • Debriefing after studies

Reflection

Importance of ethical awareness as psychology continues to evolve into the digital age, especially with AI, online therapy, and massive data collection.
